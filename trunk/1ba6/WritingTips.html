Tips for 1BA6 Papers

Catherine Bruen

Before starting to write

Writing essays is a trial and error process of recognizing critical thinking and at third level; a demonstration of independent and critical thinking is mandatory. 

Analyse the title before you start and understand every word; use a specialized dictionary if unsure. To critically assess your subject, you’ve got to think about it. You can do this by asking yourself a series of questions. Using a concept map, write down everything you know about the subject. At this early stage, be as inclusive as possible; include extra details or personal observations along the way.

Content

An abstract can prove invaluable for setting out the outline of your argument and the structure of your work. It should set out the questions you tend to raise; and the reasons you wrote what you did. It should provide a roadmap for the rest of the document so continually refer back to it so as to avoid repetition and circumlocution. Without a plan the reader loses interest in what you are saying. Once you are convinced of your own argument, you must attempt to convince the reader. Lack of depth and vague generalisations show that you yourself are not convinced.

Source all the key researchers and important works for your chosen field. Organise your material; group it according to how you intend your argument to be structured. An argument should compare and contrast the current views and consensus of the subject area in question. At this point you need to demonstrate independent thinking, and show that you’ve put some thought into the subject, simply rehashing other peoples opinions and cutting and pasting their work into some kind of structure is not acceptable. 

Once you’ve compiled all the relevant information to your argument, ask yourself how various elements fit together or if they fit at all! Sketching out your ideas will prompt a deeper exploration of the subject. At this stage, you may spot a gap, or spot different views on the same topic. Investigate the relevance of your findings, what is significant and why. What are the implications for the general area of study, how does it fit into the general consensus and more importantly, how does it rest with your own opinions and ideas. Is there anything that doesn’t fit or agree with accepted facts or theories?Ask your self how each step of the argument contributes to building a cohesive argument. Tangents and vague generalizations weaken an otherwise sound argument and are also boring for the reader. Keep in mind the relevance of what you are saying

Everything must be referenced, otherwise it is taken as plagiarism, which makes the rest of your work suspect and shakes the foundations of your whole argument. Personal experiences can add an extra dimension and depth to your work, but there is a thin line between personal opinion and irrelevant ranting…again the reader is not interested. Don’t be afraid to question and critique established thought, but avoid outright condemnation unless you can back it up. Personal views should aim to be focused, relevant to the argument and novel. But most importantly they must be leading somewhere.

Style, format, presentation

Having compiled the raw data for your argument, you’ve got to do it justice. Good ideas and strong arguments are undermined unless they can be communicated to the intended audience. You may have the courage of your convictions but you’ve got to express it to others. This is why your writing and communication style is so important.

Ask yourself is your work mostly descriptive. Does it focus on lower order thinking skills or does it demonstrate an ability to analyse and make connections. 

Are the style, structure, grammar and spelling consistent throughout.

Is the work well written and easy to read? Concise and simple language is often more effective than jargon.

Are quotes used correctly? When correctly used quotes should set the tone or mood, and support your ideas. Quotes are incorrectly used when they are over 40 words, if quoted material makes up a significant percentage of the document, when quotes drive or dominate your ideas. Do not get your quotes to write your essay for you. 

Using someone’s words or ideas without letting your reader know where they came from is a form of theft called plagiarism.[1] Plagiarism can be avoided by:

Putting the words of the author in quotation marks, recording them accurately and following eth quotation with a citation indicating your source. Common styles of Referencing are MLA, APA Yale and the Turabian.

Write a summary or paraphrase in your own words and follow it with a citation. Just changing the words does not make a paraphrase; the ideas must be digested, understood and written in your own words. It’s a good idea to lead in to your paraphrase with the author’s name. For example, “According to Nick Chapman/ Dr. Riccardo Petrella…,” followed by a quotation from Chapman or Petrella or your paraphrase of their ideas. Cite the source of anything you borrow from the Internet, including material form Web pages, e-mail and newsgroups. Be also cautious about plagiarizing your lecturers or colleagues.All citations and references should be included in the bibliography.


[1] Dr. Bryn Holmes, CRITE, Trinity College.
