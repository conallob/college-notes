General tips for writing essays

ü      Avoid sweeping generalisations. Depth is expected in one’s argument.

ü      Open with an anecdote/ quotation/interesting fact – get the reader’s attention. Don’t open with “I agree with/disagree with the statement…”

ü      Consider the meaning of the phrase “brave new world” – Huxley’s novel. Many people took ‘brave’ very literally.

ü      1500 words is short, so don’t try to cover too many topics. It’s much better to cover a few issues in depth, with examples to make your argument convincing.

ü      Don’t spend too much time/word space describing technology – your job is to analyse it.

ü      Don’t use quotes just to pad out the essay or increase the word count – they must add something to your argument.

ü      Don’t take all your quotes from one source as it will make your writing unbalanced.

ü      Keep quotes short, no more than 40-50 words. Paraphrase rather than quoting long chunks.

ü      Reference all information that is not yours or you are open to accusations of plagiarism.

ü      Put references in the form of footnotes or endnotes (perhaps just give the author’s name and/or the name of publication in the main body of your essay).  Including long references in the body of the essay can interrupt the flow of your writing.

ü      See examples on the course website for correct method of referencing.

ü      Don’t give opinions as facts e.g. ‘Knowledge is power’, ‘Information is the most important commodity’. Consider who says these things and what is at stake.

ü      Avoid value judgements – rather than saying that something is good or bad, consider who benefits / suffers because of it.

ü      In your introduction, set out what you will discuss in the course of the essay, but avoid using the personal pronoun.

ü      If your essay is well-structured, there is no need for headings in the body of the essay.

ü      Within paragraphs, have a few introductory sentences, evidence or examples of what you have said, and a concluding sentence. Paragraphs should lead into each other.

 

ü      Don’t introduce new ideas in the conclusion – this is the place to draw connections between the topics discussed and suggest areas for further research.

ü      Keep sentences short and clear. Avoid using very long sentences as meaning can be obscured.

ü      Proof read – spell check is not enough! It may help to read your essay aloud or get somebody else to read it as they are more likely to spot errors.

ü      Common spelling mistakes – unnecessary apostrophes (e.g “Computer’s are great…”); confusing its and it’s (its for possession, it’s for ‘it is’ – “It’s funny when the dog wags its tail”); mixing up whose and who’s (who’s=who is, whose for ownership – “Who’s the person whose bag is here?”).
