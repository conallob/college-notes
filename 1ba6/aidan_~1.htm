The Internet and the Information Age

Fundamental Paradigm Shift or More of the Same

 

 

Abstract

 

In this essay I aim to pose three main questions, which (in this very broad topic) are of interest to me.  The first question I ask is “does the Internet Age really herald free access to information for all?”. Secondly, “does the high-tech industry operate within a context of moral concern for the welfare of its customers and society as a whole?”.  And thirdly, “how will technology shape society and economy in the 21st Century?”. I have posed the questions in such a way that they cannot be answered with absolute certainty, but instead I hope that this essay will give the reader enough food for thought to make up his or her own mind on the issue.

 

Question the First

 

“Does the Internet Age really herald free access to information for all?”

 

Let us be honest: we are spontaneously sympathetic to the idea that we are moving towards an information society, even before taking the facts into consideration. This notion is pleasing to us, not only because its usual descriptions evoke the idea of a society in which knowledge and information will be readily accessible and greatly shared (i.e., a more just, more prosperous, and more democratic society), but because it comforts us in our own personal choices: we (as technologists) really are engaged in important matters, we who have chosen to devote our professional lives to the study of communication. We were right. Our work is at the heart of the changes traversing contemporary societies.

 

Yet is this sympathy justly founded or is it a form of complacency on the part of the technologist? Despite all or any denials, we (as technologists) find it difficult to distance ourselves from technological determinism. We are spontaneously given to according considerable and at times inordinate importance to communication media. We tend to begin with the technological, to analyse its characteristics, and to study or deduce its social, economic, cultural, and political consequences. Too often, our work implicitly assumes that technology has an autonomous, independent status relative to socio-economic structures and actions of “social actors”.  I think this tendency to situate technology at the centre of our work needs to be questioned, particularly since we go so far as to draw general conclusions about the type of society in which we live or which we foretell.

 

The dominant discourse on communication technologies, particularly on what is called “the new information and communication technologies” (NICTs), is characterized by an optimism, which, though not always perfect nor without nuance, is nevertheless fundamentally convinced that we are progressing. The least one can say is that Jacques Ellul has been left aside! [1] And one hardly ever hears, with respect to communication technologies, the same criticisms and doubts abundantly expressed in every other field by the ecology movement. Indeed, communication technology is considered to be essentially positive: it does not pollute, can replace transportation, even allow the movement to organize itself, etc. This optimism is tempered only by the threat posed to individual privacy by the establishment of all manner of electronic files.

 

But these points themselves can be seriously questioned. Is this new communication technology really as clean as we think? One only has to focus for a minute on the “Car-Computer Analogy” put forward by Cling [2] to see that perhaps we are jumping the gun with regards to holding computers in a position of near deity.

 

“When motor cars first became popular…they were viewed as a clean technology. Larger cities had annoying pollution problems from another primary transportation technology, horses. … By the 1960s we were beginning to view cars as a major polluting technology, since smog visibly dirtied the air of major cities” [2]

 

So the question has to be raised: is there some, as yet unforeseen, problem inherent with computers that will one day become only all too obvious, and at too late a stage?

 

Whatever about agreeing or disagreeing with the above, the original question I asked could be taken to an even more basic level – not everyone has access to the Internet in the first place. In fact “not everyone” is a gross understatement.  Only 2% of the world’s population are connected to the Internet! [3] That is quite astounding considering its power and scope. So how can there possibly be free information for all (in the true sense of the expression), if such a small number of people are connected? Furthermore, 57% of Internet users are from the USA – a country that makes up only 4.7% of the world’s population [4]. Furthermore, only 21.7% of Internet users live outside USA and Europe [5] and take into account that Japan and Australia make up a considerable percentage of this. So if free information for all means that the richest in society get richer thanks to this information, then yes there is free information for all. But if free information for all means that a fundamental paradigm shift in the outlook of society is achieved by connecting all humans to the net, regardless of location, race, or social status, then we are still gulfs away from this utopia.

 

Question 2

 

“Does the high-tech industry operate within a context of moral concern for the health and welfare of its customers and the society of end users?”

 

In an old issue of Daedalus magazine, I came across this remark by Joseph Weizenbaum [6] (addressed to a doctor as part of an informal discussion):

 

“You were taught what to do if a patient were to walk into your office and say, "Doctor, I want you to amputate my little finger, and how much do you charge for that?" You would not do what the technologist does, that is, ask..."What are the specifications? Do I have the resources? Do I have the competence?" and if all these questions are answered appropriately, say that you will perform the amputation. Instead, you take ... responsibility for finding as best you can what the problem is. You may decide that what is really necessary is aspirin or bedrest, or an amputation of the foot, and you will behave accordingly, quite independent of how much the patient is willing to pay you to cut off his little finger. That is what you were taught as a physician. That is precisely the opposite of what happens in almost all engineering practice.”

 

This extremely interesting extract points out that a doctor, of course, acts within a world of concern that includes the health and welfare of the patient. This lends an inescapably moral quality to the treatment. It is clear to the reader that while a doctor does what is best for his patient regardless of what the doctor can gain otherwise, an IT professional approaches the situation in the exact opposite manner. That is, the IT professional looks for a way to get the most money out of his customer, regardless of there being a solution which is either cheaper or better for society.

 

Now I realise that perhaps this is selling IT professionals short as nothing more that money-grabbing know-it-alls, but there is undoubtedly a valid point raised in this argument. Are our IT professionals being trained in the wrong way? Should there be a fundamental overhaul in the way in which our IT professionals are trained, so that society as a whole can reap the benefits?

 

 

Question 3

 

“How will technology shape society and economy in the 21st Century?”

 

There have been many attempts to name the changes to post-Second World War society and to identify the resulting new society. These have included “mass society”, “programmed society”, ”post-industrial society”, “consumer society”, “information society” and so on. If certain authors are to be believed, society types change as often as governments! Historians will no doubt sort matters out in a few decades. But despite the fantastic acceleration of the rate of change over the last half century, profound transformations of society are less rapid and certain developments, hailed as fundamental, will not have as dramatic an effect on the system’s base as had been anticipated.

 

Undoubtedly society and the economy will be fundamentally different from just about everything we’ve known for the past 300 or so years. For the first time, we are creating a technological civilization that is not industrial. It’s not another stage of industrialism. It’s not simply “post-industrial” or “post-modern”. To call something “post-anything” is not descriptive. It just locates the phenomenon in linear time, but it doesn’t tell you anything else about it. What we’re living through is the rapid emergence and spread of a whole new way of life, and a totally new way of creating wealth.

 

Yet I’m not sure if it’s fair to say that technology is shaping our future. Culture, religion, family structure, social institutions and values – all are in the process of changing each other through complex feedback with one another. And technology is part of this overall interactive system. Technology impacts our values. But the reverse is just as true. For example, our value system determines where we put our research money. Do we pump it all into low-risk, commercially oriented research and development, or do we support basic research? Our values as a society determine the answers. But technology, in turn, once developed and applied, reshapes values. So it seems to be somewhat of a catch-22 situation.

 

In the future I don’t think one can say that technology alone will be the main moulding device. In our society, business will (and already does) play a powerful lead role. In part, that’s because of its political influence. But it also has to do with disparate rates of institutional changes in society. Compared with the educational system, or, for that matter, the political system, business changes most quickly and flexibly. Because of factors such as competition, business is compelled to change very rapidly if it’s going to survive and meet its short-term profit goals. Moreover, the acceleration of change, which affects everyone, is an especially powerful factor in business. And business in turn produces endless innovations whose sole purpose is to save time and make events move even faster. So clearly business has the potential to shape how we invest in our technology. But ultimately, one could argue that the main factor steering business is consumer demand and thus it is the consumer that will, in the 21st Century, shape the technological drive that will shape our society!

 

I believe it is pointless in speculating on what innovations will actually be realised as the coming decades unfold – this is merely something fun and sensational for the media to speculate about. We should know by now that in the past speculators have often gotten it so very wrong. Take Christopher Evan’s “The Mighty Micro” [7] for example. In 1979 it predicted that within 15 years there would be:

 

“… speaking bathroom scales, freezers which remind you to restock them, cookers which tell you how the meat is coming along, telephones that tell you how many people have rung in your absence, door bells which inform you how many visitors you have had; thermometers that advise you what to wear before you get up”

 

Furthermore, in the second chapter (“New Highways”) of James Martin’s 1978 book, “The Wired Society” [8], an idyllic society is depicted:

 

“There is almost no street robbery, because most persons carry little cash.... Citizens can wear radio devices for automatically calling police or ambulances if they wish.”

 

“Imagine a city ten or twenty years in the future, with parks and flowers and lakes, where the air is crystal clear and most cars are kept in large parking lots on the outskirts. The high-rise buildings are not too close, so they all have good views, and everyone living in the city can walk through the gardens or rain-free pedestrian mall to shops, restaurants, or pubs. The city has cabling under the streets and new forms of radio that provide all manner of communication facilities. The television sets, which can pick up many more channels than today’s television, can also be used in conjunction with small keyboards to provide a multitude of communication services. The more affluent citizens have 7-foot television screens, or even larger.”

 

What ever about these predictions being totally incorrect (in fact all of the above ARE probably technically possible), there is arguably a lacking of need for the above inventions so they just become some wonderful fantasy. There is also nothing that might allow for anticipating the events in Oklahoma City. Nor is there anything about the infatuation of television viewers with events like the O. J. Simpson trial rather than with city council debates. What lovely optimism! What lovely naïveté! What utopianism!

 

I while I obviously can’t tell you what the world of tomorrow will be like, I will hazard a guess that it will be nothing like the one predicted by the eulogists of the information society. They provide us with a globalising but ultimately simplistic model. If communication theorists wish to participate in the effort to understand society as a whole and the transformations traversing it, they will truly have to come to grips with its complexity and propose something other than a determinist vision centred only on technology and its effects.

 

Conclusion

 

In conclusion, for the Internet to become more than just “more of the same”, businesses, technocrats and also world leaders must work together to develop a definite strategy. At the moment I believe that the Internet is still a technology in search of a strategy. Companies need a strategic framework that can bridge the gap between simply connecting to the Internet and harnessing its power for competitive advantage. In order for a business to develop a logical strategy for Internet utilization there must be a mechanism utilized that will allow the Internet community easy access to that business's products, services, and information.

 

We must assess whether or not the Information Revolution really is a multi-layered revolution which spans society, race, class, etc or whether it is merely a device to facilitate an elite few. We must ask “does free information for all” really mean what it says. And we must also ask ourselves if our technological strategies really are in the interests of society – or are they just a means of making a quick buck.
