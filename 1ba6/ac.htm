Introduction:

In this essay, I shall show how society has been influenced by IT and how it will continue to shape our society using education as a prime example. I will then analyse some possibilities on how education will change in an attempt to integrate this new technology into the educational sector. Secondly I shall analyse previous technological trends and theories and attempt to apply these to IT to extrapolate the impact social change will have in the Information Age.

Education:

In the Age of Information, how exactly will social structure be influenced? As communication becomes ever faster and cheaper with the advent of electronic mail and the Internet, changes in society are inevitable. Enormous amounts of information can be freely accessed, businesses and stock markets can quickly exchange large sums of money electronically and the workplace has expanded outside the domain of the office and into devices such as laptops and mobile phones. No longer is distance an objective, and change must take place in order to integrate this new technology into key aspects of society.
One important sector that has remained relatively unchanged with the rise of IT is the educational sector. In fact new technology such as computers and the Internet, seem more of a burden in schools, then as a revolutionary instrument to greatly aid education. Although there has been an increase in the number of computers recently in schools, the educational system lacks the proper time and resources to fully integrate the computer and IT into the educational system. Teachers must be trained in modern IT skills, and also with new strategies to be able to integrate IT effectively into the curriculum. Technical support must be also available. Funding computers for schools will not solve the problem, if they cannot be fully integrated in the educational system. In effect, the educational sector has failed in this respect. As IT develops further, the old education system may need to be uprooted and a new foundation based on dynamic-learning set.
William H. Dutton in his "Information and Communication Technologies" makes an interesting point in relation to education in IT. He suggests that in technology areas education will be not be terminated by a degree or university qualification, but rather a lifelong learning will be adapted, as qualifications will become outdated within a few years. He assumes modular courses will become something of the past and a "meltdown" in education will occur. [1] This statement would seem true with the accelerating improvements in technology, and adds to the argument that the educational sector needs to be seriously looked at.
A very interesting and topical book I came across by Frederick Bennett called "Computers as Tutors: Solving the crisis in education", discusses in-depth the ideas of computers in education. Bennett boldly suggests removing schools and even teachers from education and argues the idea as computers and IT as the new educators. He makes strong arguments for his ideas and some of the advantages he mentions such as individualised learning and grade-less learning based reward system seem intriguing. [2] With the use of Media on the Internet such as audio and visual, it has become an even greater educational tool, as interaction is a greater learning tool than reading from a book.


Technological Trends and Theories:

It is impossible to predict the impact IT will have on society in the future, but by analysing previous technological advancements and their effect, we can make a somewhat educated assumption.
Arnult Grübler argues in his book "Technology and Global Change", that no individual technological creation can be so important as to create a "global change" - it is the use society makes of this technology that creates the change. [3] With reference to Kling's "Car-Computer Analogy", we can see that this is true. Urbanisation and the increase of fossil fuels were not caused by the invention of the automobile or the steam engine, but rather the application of these inventions by social choice. [4] By applying this idea to IT, we can gradually see a pattern, and the social changes emerging from society's choice to embrace these new technologies. Already we can see changes in the workplace, employment, education and health. Both the rail and the car took around a hundred years to reach maturity and ultimately technological saturation. As IT continues to grow and diffuse, will it in turn shape society further with it? Previous trends in technological advancement would assume that this is indeed so.
By comparing the car to IT maybe we can see some possible consequences and problems that may arise as we head further into the Information Age. Sean O'Connell's book "The Car in British Society" provides a good background on this. O'Connell states that the design, marketing and uses of cars were affected by social, cultural and economical factors and in turn this impacted social aspects such as class and gender in relation to the car. [5] At one stage, computers and the Internet were only accessibly by those who could afford them, but as computers reduced in price, like the car, they became more accessibly to the majority. One striking idea O'Connell proposes is that as the levels of ownership increase, so opinion is swayed against restrictions placed on the technological invention. The consequences of death on the roads are accepted as natural and inevitable to modern society. Society is blinded by the high rate of death on the roads and assumes that increasing road safety measures and issuing speed limits can reduce this rate. Robert Davis sums this idea up nicely in his book "Death on the streets":
"Idealogy sets hidden agendas of discussion, forms background assumptions about the nature of those discussions and above all, makes certain features of society appear natural or necessary" [6]
As consequences of IT, we can already take for example September 11th. Will incidents like these become socially acceptable as by-products of further IT development?
The idea of technological determinism seems somewhat one-sided, that advancements in technologies will continue to shape society. Would it not be truer to assume that society influences the growth and integration of technology and in turn technology influences society? Wiebe Byker and John Law in their book "Shaping Technology/Building Society" put forward an interesting view, which I have taken keenly to. They suggest an alternative view called the "Interactive view" on how technology and society are inter-related. They suggest a "matter of fact" distinction between the society and technology and argue the "interaction and exchange" between the two, instead of the assumptions of either social or technological determinism. [7] This view seems more logical to me upon analysing technological trends, but opens up the possibility that maybe we cannot accurately predict the outcome of social change in relation to advancements in technology. By looking at Klings "Car-Computer Analogy" again, we can see the unpredicted consequences of the car - it was firstly issued as a "clean technology", but that is not the case today. [4]


Conclusion:

As I look at the ever-changing times, and then at the outdated educational system, I can only but wonder what will happen to it. Will it be swept away by the IT revolution and in its place, computerised teachers and schools or will it remain as it always has for so long, caught in the old methodology of teacher to class learning? Can we afford to keep this system going? Unless education is reconsidered, the future for education looks bleak. As lifetime learning becomes a reality and information becomes more easily and freely accessible, will the whole notion of education be done away with? Like the car, IT seems to be a wonderful, advantageous, beneficial technology, but will IT harbour unforeseen consequences as the car has? As access to technology becomes free to all, will incidents like September 11th become the norm and socially acceptable as consequences of our new technologies? The future ahead seems both exciting and scary, and I agree that IT is the key that will open the door, into a brave and interesting new world.
